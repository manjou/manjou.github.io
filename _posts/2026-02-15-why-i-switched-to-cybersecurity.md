I spent most of my career in operations: managing café teams, processing property documentation, handling customer cases at scale. Not an obvious path to cybersecurity. But looking back, the connection is clearer than I thought.

## What Operations Taught Me

Working in high-volume, audit-logged environments taught me something that's actually core to security work: **process matters**. When you're managing 80+ floor plan reviews daily, or reconciling POS transactions, you develop a muscle for following procedures precisely, catching anomalies, and escalating when something doesn't fit the pattern.

That's not so different from what a Tier 1 SOC analyst does: triage alerts, follow runbooks, escalate anything that looks unusual.

The pivot point for me was recognizing that I already had the operational discipline. What I needed was the technical foundation.

## The Development Detour

Before cybersecurity, I did a full-stack development bootcamp at CareerFoundry. That was genuinely useful — not because I became a developer, but because understanding how web applications work (authentication, APIs, databases, session management) gives you an enormous advantage when you're analyzing logs or investigating incidents involving web applications.

When I see a JWT in an alert, I know what it is. When Wazuh flags an unusual port 27017 connection, I know that's MongoDB and I know why that's interesting.

## Why Cybersecurity Specifically

I did a lot of research before committing. The things that drew me in:

- **The work is genuinely investigative.** You're reconstructing what happened from incomplete evidence. That appeals to the same part of me that liked operational problem-solving.
- **The field rewards continuous learning.** You can't coast on old knowledge. Threat actors evolve, so defenders have to evolve too.
- **Entry paths are more accessible than I expected.** The certification ecosystem (CompTIA especially) gives you a structured way to demonstrate baseline knowledge without a CS degree.

## Where I Am Now

I'm about a year into the Masterschool Cybersecurity Training Program. I passed Security+ in January 2026. I've built a home lab running Wazuh, and I'm finishing the Aurora AI Security capstone project.

The transition is taking longer than I hoped — that's honest. But every lab session where I generate an alert, investigate it, and understand *why* Wazuh fired is a small proof point that this is the right direction.

If you're thinking about a similar move, feel free to reach out. I'm happy to share what's worked and what hasn't.
